⭐ H-1B — Specialty Occupation Visa

The most widely used US work visa. Employer-sponsored, subject to annual lottery, grants dual intent. Major cost increase since Sept. 2025 Presidential Proclamation.

Duration
Up to 6 years
Annual Cap
85,000
Start Date
October 1
Registration Fee
$215 (FY2026)
+ Proclamation Fee
$100,000 (2025+)
🚨 Presidential Proclamation — Sept. 19, 2025: Employers must pay an additional $100,000 per H-1B petition filed on or after Sept. 21, 2025 (new petitions, extensions, amendments, COS). Payment must be via pay.gov before filing. USCIS Source →
FY2027 Change: Weighted lottery selection (effective Feb. 27, 2026) will favor higher-wage/skill beneficiaries starting FY2027 cap season.

✅ Employee Requirements

  • Bachelor's degree or higher (or equivalent) in a specialty field directly related to the job
  • The position must qualify as a "specialty occupation" — normally requiring at least a bachelor's
  • Foreign degree equivalency accepted (credential evaluation)
  • For fashion models: distinguished merit and ability

🏢 Employer Requirements

  • Valid US employer with FEIN
  • Must offer a bona fide specialty occupation role
  • Must pay the higher of prevailing wage or actual wage
  • Must file LCA with DOL before petition
  • Must demonstrate ability to pay the offered wage
  • Must pay $100,000 additional fee (Sept. 2025+)

📋 Specialty Occupation Examples

  • Software engineers, IT specialists, data scientists
  • Financial analysts, accountants, economists
  • Architects, civil/mechanical/electrical engineers
  • Doctors, physical therapists (with separate licensure)
  • Lawyers, market research analysts
  • University professors and researchers

⚡ Cap-Exempt Employers

  • Institutions of higher education (universities, colleges)
  • Nonprofits affiliated with or related to universities
  • Nonprofit or governmental research organizations
  • Cap-exempt employers can file year-round — no lottery needed

H-1B Process — Step by Step

1

Secure Employer Sponsorship

A US employer agrees to sponsor. Must have a bona fide specialty occupation job offer and willingness to pay all required fees.

Timing: Before Feb registration window
2

File Labor Condition Application (LCA) with DOL

Employer files via DOL's FLAG system, attesting to prevailing wage, working conditions, and no strike/lockout. Must be certified before I-129 filing.

Fee: None (DOL)Processing: ~7 business days
3

Online Registration with USCIS (Lottery)

Employer registers each beneficiary during the annual window (~March 1–20). Fee: $215/beneficiary. If registrations exceed cap, USCIS conducts lottery.

FY2026 registered: ~344,000 unique beneficiaries
4

Lottery Results

USCIS selects registrations. Under the beneficiary-centric model (since FY2025), each person can only be selected once regardless of multiple employer submissions. Notified late March.

5

Pay $100,000 Proclamation Fee (pay.gov)

For petitions on/after Sept. 21, 2025 — must pay before filing. Proof of scheduled payment required with I-129. Narrow national-interest exceptions only.

Fee: $100,000 per petition
6

File Form I-129 with USCIS

Selected employers file I-129 with certified LCA, employee credentials, job offer letter, and ability-to-pay proof. 90-day filing window opens April 1.

Base fee: $730 + ACWIA + fraud + asylum feesPremium: +$2,965 for 15-day processing
7

USCIS Adjudication

USCIS reviews; may issue RFE. If approved, I-797 Notice issued. Premium Processing guarantees 15-day response (not final approval if RFE issued).

Standard: 3–6 monthsPremium: 15 business days
8

Visa Stamp (if abroad) / Status Begins Oct. 1

If outside the US, apply for H-1B visa stamp at US consulate with I-797. If in valid US status, change of status is effective October 1.

MRV fee: $185Start date: October 1
9

Extensions & AC21 Beyond 6 Years

Initial approval: 3 years, extendable to 6. With pending/approved I-140 and PERM filed 365+ days, extensions beyond 6 years available in 1- or 3-year increments.

⚖️ Legal Note: Consult a licensed immigration attorney. USCIS H-1B Official Page →

EB Green Card Process (EB-2/EB-3 Standard — with PERM)

1

Prevailing Wage Determination (PWD)

Employer submits ETA-9141 to DOL's NPWC to get the official prevailing wage for the role/location. This sets the wage floor for the green card applicant.

Timeline: 6–8 months avgFee: None (DOL)
2

PERM Labor Certification (ETA-9089)

Employer conducts formal recruitment (job posting, newspaper ads, website, etc.) for ~60–90 days proving no qualified US worker is available. Files ETA-9089 with DOL. Audits add 6–12+ months.

DOL adjudication: 10–16+ months (avg ~499 days as of Dec 2025)Ad costs: ~$1,000–3,000
3

File I-140 Immigrant Petition

After PERM approval, employer files I-140 with USCIS. Locks in the priority date for the Visa Bulletin queue. Approved I-140 survives employer changes under AC21 after 180 days.

Standard: 6–12 monthsPremium: 15 days (45 days EB-1C/NIW)Fee: $715 + $600 Asylum Program
4

Wait for Priority Date (Visa Bulletin)

State Dept publishes monthly Visa Bulletins showing which dates are current. India and China nationals may wait years or decades. Monitor travel.state.gov monthly.

Rest of World EB-1: Current (no wait)India EB-2: ~13–15 years
5

File I-485 Adjustment of Status (or Consular Processing)

If in US: file I-485 when priority date is current. Simultaneously file I-765 (EAD) and I-131 (advance parole). If abroad: DS-260 for consular processing. Medical exam (I-693) required.

I-485 fee: $1,440 (incl. biometrics)DS-260 fee: $345Processing: 8–18 months
6

Biometrics Appointment

USCIS schedules biometrics (fingerprints, photo, signature) ~4–8 weeks after I-485 receipt for background checks at an Application Support Center (ASC).

7

Interview (if required) & Decision

Many employment-based cases are interview-waived. If required, interview at USCIS field office. USCIS approves, issues RFE, or issues NOID.

Total I-485 processing: 8–18 months
8

Green Card Issued

Form I-551 (Permanent Resident Card) mailed after approval. Valid 10 years; renewable. Citizenship eligibility after 5 years (3 years if married to US citizen).

⚖️ EB-1A, EB-1B, and EB-2 NIW skip Steps 1–2 (no PERM required). Source: USCIS Employment Green Card →

⚡ AC21 Portability

  • After I-485 pending 180+ days, you can change employers or job roles
  • New job must be "same or similar occupational classification"
  • Approved I-140 stays valid for priority date purposes even if employer withdraws after 180 days
  • Always consult attorney before using portability

🏃 Strategies to Accelerate

  • File PERM as early as possible to lock in priority date
  • Pursue EB-1A or EB-2 NIW if qualifications support it (no PERM)
  • Dual-file I-140 in EB-2 and EB-3 for flexibility
  • Use premium I-140 to establish priority date faster
  • Monitor Visa Bulletin — file I-485 as soon as eligible

⚠ Common Pitfalls

  • Not starting PERM early enough before H-1B 6-year limit
  • Employer withdrawing I-140 before 180-day threshold
  • Travel outside US without Advance Parole after I-485 filing (abandons application)
  • Missing Visa Bulletin windows — act fast when dates become current

🏢 L-1 — Intracompany Transferee

For multinational companies transferring executives, managers (L-1A) or specialized knowledge employees (L-1B) from a foreign affiliate to a US office. No annual cap, no lottery, dual intent allowed.

L-1A Max
7 years
L-1B Max
5 years
Initial Stay
3 yrs (1 yr new office)
Cap / Lottery
None ✓

L-1A — Executives & Managers

  • Transferring to executive or managerial role at US entity
  • Must have worked 12 continuous months (in last 3 years) abroad at related entity in exec/manager capacity
  • US and foreign entities must share at least 50% common ownership
  • Initial 3 years; extendable to 7 years total
  • Natural EB-1C green card pathway available

L-1B — Specialized Knowledge

  • Must possess specialized knowledge of company's products, services, techniques, or procedures
  • Same 12-month abroad employment requirement
  • Initial 3 years; extendable to 5 years total (max)
  • Must leave US for 1 year to reset after max stay
  • Harder to prove "specialized" vs. "general" knowledge — document carefully

Key Advantages

  • No annual cap or lottery — file anytime
  • Dual intent — pursue green card simultaneously
  • Spouse (L-2) is employment-authorized
  • L-1A → EB-1C green card: no PERM required
  • Blanket L for large companies streamlines process
  • Canadians can apply at the border

Process & Costs

  • Employer files Form I-129 with USCIS
  • Standard: 3–5 months; Premium: 15 business days (~$2,965)
  • I-129 base fee: $730 (+ fraud fee $500 for some)
  • MRV visa stamp fee (abroad): $185
  • New office petitions: initial 1-year approval only

🏆 O-1 — Extraordinary Ability / Achievement

For individuals who have risen to the very top of their field. No annual cap, no lottery, no degree requirement. O-1A covers sciences, business, education, and athletics. O-1B covers arts, film, and television. ~94% approval rate for well-prepared petitions.

Initial Duration
Up to 3 years
Extensions
1-yr increments, unlimited
Cap / Lottery
None ✓
Approval Rate
~94% (FY2025)
Premium Processing
15 business days
💡 "Genius Visa": While often associated with celebrities, the O-1 is increasingly used by startup founders, researchers, engineers, scientists, and senior business leaders who can demonstrate exceptional impact in their field — not just fame.

O-1A Criteria (Sciences, Business, Education, Athletics)

Must meet at least 3 of the following 8 criteria, or show a one-time major award (Nobel, Olympic medal, etc.):

  • Receipt of nationally/internationally recognized prizes or awards for excellence
  • Membership in associations requiring outstanding achievements (judged by experts)
  • Published material about the individual in major trade/professional publications or media
  • Participation as a judge of others' work (individual or on a panel)
  • Original scientific, scholarly, or business contributions of major significance
  • Authorship of scholarly articles in professional journals or major media
  • Critical/essential role for organizations with distinguished reputations
  • Command of a high salary or remuneration relative to others in the field

O-1B Criteria (Arts, Film & TV)

Must demonstrate "distinction" — a high level of achievement substantially above that ordinarily encountered, evidenced by meeting at least 3 criteria:

  • Lead/starring role in productions with distinguished reputation
  • Critical/essential role for organizations or establishments with distinguished reputation
  • Record of major commercial or critically acclaimed successes
  • Significant recognition from organizations, critics, government agencies, or experts
  • High salary/remuneration relative to others in the field
  • For film/TV: additional criteria around recognition from peers, award nominations, etc.

Process Overview

  • US employer or agent files Form I-129 on behalf of the beneficiary
  • Self-petitioning is not allowed — a US sponsor is required
  • Agents can represent beneficiaries working for multiple employers
  • Required: consultation letter from a peer group, labor union, or management organization
  • Include: evidence package, recommendation letters, itinerary, employment contract
  • Standard processing: 2.5–6 months; Premium: 15 business days
  • USCIS O-1 Official Page →

Fees & Key Facts

  • I-129 base fee: $1,055 (large employers) / $530 (nonprofits)
  • Asylum Program Fee: $600 (large) / $300 (small ≤25 FTE)
  • Premium Processing: $2,965 for 15-day response
  • New $250 visa integrity fee (FY2025+) at issuance
  • Consultation letter fee: ~$250–750
  • Attorney fees: $5,000–$15,000 typical; $15,000–$25,000 for complex O-1B
  • O-1 is employer-specific; new employer must file new I-129
  • Spouse/children (O-3): may accompany but cannot work

✅ Advantages

  • No cap, no lottery — can file any time of year
  • No degree requirement — achievement-based, not education-based
  • Dual intent (de facto): can pursue green card while on O-1
  • Unlimited 1-year extensions as long as work/project continues
  • Can hold concurrent O-1s for multiple employers/projects (via agent)
  • Strong alternative to H-1B lottery for qualified professionals

⚠ Limitations

  • High evidentiary bar — requires comprehensive documentation strategy
  • Self-petitioning not allowed — need a US employer or agent
  • Visa is employer/project specific — changing jobs requires new petition
  • O-3 dependents cannot work (unlike H-4 EAD or L-2 EAD)
  • Evidence preparation can take 1–4 months if starting from scratch
Source: USCIS O-1 Page · Deel O-1 Guide

📚 F-1 — Academic Student Visa

The primary visa for full-time academic study at US universities, colleges, seminaries, conservatories, and SEVP-certified language programs. Gateway to OPT/CPT work authorization and the most common path for international students.

Program Duration
Duration of Status (D/S)*
SEVIS Fee
$350
Visa Application Fee
$185 (DS-160)
Work Auth
OPT / CPT post-year-1
⚠ 2025 Policy Update: DHS has moved away from "Duration of Status" (D/S) admission toward fixed-period stays (up to 4 years for most programs), requiring USCIS extension for longer programs. Social media vetting is now conducted for all F/J/M applicants. Consult your Designated School Official (DSO) for current guidance.

Eligibility Requirements

  • Accepted by an SEVP-certified school (receives Form I-20 from school)
  • Enrolled full-time (typically 12+ credit hours/semester for undergrad)
  • Sufficient financial resources to cover full cost of attendance (tuition + living)
  • English proficiency (or enrolled in English language program)
  • Strong ties to home country demonstrating intent to return after studies
  • Must maintain full-time status — dropping below requires DSO authorization

Application Process

  • Step 1: Apply to and get accepted by SEVP-certified school
  • Step 2: School issues Form I-20 (Certificate of Eligibility)
  • Step 3: Pay SEVIS fee ($350) at fmjfee.com
  • Step 4: Complete DS-160 online visa application
  • Step 5: Pay MRV visa application fee ($185)
  • Step 6: Schedule and attend consular interview at US embassy/consulate
  • Step 7: Bring visa + I-20 to US port of entry — admitted by CBP
  • Apply at least 3–4 months before program start date

Work Authorization Rules

  • Year 1: On-campus work only (up to 20 hrs/week during term; full-time on breaks)
  • After Year 1: CPT or OPT available for off-campus work related to major
  • Economic hardship: off-campus work possible with DSO/USCIS authorization
  • Unauthorized employment is a serious status violation — can trigger SEVIS termination and removal
  • Must maintain 20 hrs/week minimum if OPT/CPT employer requires active enrollment

Family & Key Facts

  • Spouse and children under 21: eligible for F-2 dependent visa
  • F-2 spouses: may study part-time only, cannot work
  • F-2 children: may attend K–12 full-time
  • 60-day grace period after graduation/OPT end to change status or depart
  • Transfer to another SEVP-certified school: follow official SEVIS transfer process

💼 OPT & CPT — Student Work Authorization

Two work authorization programs for F-1 students. OPT (Optional Practical Training) is post-graduation work authorization: 12 months standard, 36 months for STEM graduates. CPT (Curricular Practical Training) is work integrated into the curriculum before graduation.

OPT Standard
12 months
OPT STEM Extension
+24 months = 36 total
CPT
Pre-graduation, curriculum-tied
I-765 Fee
$410

OPT — Optional Practical Training

  • Allows F-1 students to work in a job directly related to their major field of study
  • 12 months post-completion (or pre-completion for up to 12 months, deducted from total)
  • STEM graduates (qualifying degree + E-Verify employer): +24-month extension = 36 months total
  • Application: Request OPT recommendation from DSO ≥ 90 days before desired start
  • File Form I-765 with USCIS; processing: ~3–5 months — apply early
  • STEM extension employer must be enrolled in E-Verify and file Form I-983 training plan

CPT — Curricular Practical Training

  • Work that is an integral part of the student's curriculum (internships, co-ops, practicums)
  • Available during studies — does not require USCIS filing; authorized by DSO on Form I-20
  • Must be directly tied to the curriculum (often required by the degree program)
  • Full-time CPT for 12+ months reduces standard OPT eligibility to zero — plan carefully
  • If you use CPT full-time for ≥12 months, you forfeit post-completion OPT entirely

Cap-Gap Protection (OPT → H-1B)

  • If an H-1B petition is filed before OPT EAD expires, "cap-gap" extends F-1/OPT status until October 1
  • STEM OPT holders: work authorization continues through October 1 during cap-gap
  • Non-STEM OPT holders: may need to stop working until Oct 1 if EAD expires before that date
  • Do not leave the US during cap-gap without a valid H-1B visa or advance planning

Key Rules & Limits

  • Employment must be ≥20 hrs/week and directly related to degree field
  • Unemployment limit: 90 days total (non-STEM); 150 days total (STEM OPT)
  • 60-day grace period after OPT ends to change status or depart
  • Working outside your field of study violates status
  • Apply for OPT early — USCIS processing can take 3–5 months and delays are common
  • USCIS OPT Page →

✈️ B-1/B-2 — Business Visitor & Tourist Visa

B-1 is for temporary business activities (meetings, conferences, negotiations, training). B-2 is for tourism, vacation, and medical treatment. Frequently issued as a combined B-1/B-2 visa. Widely available to most nationalities, and many countries qualify for visa-free entry via the Visa Waiver Program (VWP/ESTA).

Typical Stay
Up to 6 months
Visa Validity
Up to 10 years (multiple entry)
Work Authorized
No
VWP/ESTA
38 countries, 90-day max
🚫 No Work Permitted: B-1/B-2 visa holders (and ESTA/VWP visitors) are strictly prohibited from working in the US for compensation. This includes remote work for a foreign employer while physically in the US (legally ambiguous, but generally treated as work). Violations can result in removal and future inadmissibility.

B-1 — Permitted Business Activities

  • Attending business meetings, conferences, and seminars
  • Negotiating contracts on behalf of a foreign employer
  • Conducting market research or independent research
  • Short-term training (receiving training, not working/performing services)
  • Participating in scientific, educational, professional, or business conventions
  • May NOT receive US-source salary or compensation
  • May NOT perform hands-on work or deliver services to a US client

B-2 — Permitted Activities

  • Tourism, vacation, sightseeing
  • Visiting friends or relatives
  • Medical treatment
  • Participation in amateur events (no pay)
  • Short recreational courses (non-credit, incidental)
  • May NOT enroll in full-time academic or language study programs
  • May NOT work in any capacity

Application Process

  • Complete Form DS-160 (online nonimmigrant visa application)
  • Pay MRV application fee: $185
  • Schedule and attend consular interview at US embassy/consulate
  • Bring: passport, DS-160 confirmation, photo, financial evidence, ties to home country
  • CBP officer at port of entry determines length of authorized stay (typically up to 6 months)
  • Extension: file Form I-539 with USCIS before current status expires
  • State Dept B Visa Page →

Visa Waiver Program (ESTA)

  • Citizens of 38 countries (UK, Germany, France, Japan, Australia, etc.) can enter without a visa under VWP for up to 90 days
  • Must apply for ESTA authorization online at esta.cbp.dhs.gov (fee: $21)
  • Same activity restrictions as B-1/B-2 — no work permitted
  • Cannot extend beyond 90 days or change to most other visa statuses from within the US
  • Overstaying VWP/ESTA — even by 1 day — can result in permanent bar from future VWP use
  • ESTA Application →
